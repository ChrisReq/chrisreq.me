## Why GDPR Matters for E-Learning Tools

As a teacher or trainer, you are legally responsible for the tools you use in your sessions. It sounds complicated, but it's actually straightforward: if a tool transfers personal data from your students — even just names or IP addresses — to the US, you need a legal basis for that transfer. And since the Schrems II ruling in 2020, that basis is effectively gone.

What this means in practice: if you use Kahoot in a school setting and student data flows to US servers, you as the instructor can be held personally accountable. Many schools and universities have already introduced internal policies that ban or severely restrict US based tools.

The good news: there are now excellent alternatives that are fully hosted in the EU and built from the ground up with GDPR compliance in mind.

## The 5 Key Criteria for GDPR Compliant Learning Tools

Before we dive into the comparison, here are the criteria that actually matter:

- **EU server location** — Data must not be transferred to third countries without an adequate level of data protection.
- **No registration required for participants** — If students don't need to create an account, no personal data is generated in the first place.
- **Data Processing Agreement (DPA) available** — A DPA is legally required for schools and companies.
- **No tracking or advertising** — Third party cookies and tracking pixels are a clear no go.
- **Transparent privacy policy** — As an educator, you should be able to understand exactly what data is being collected.

## Mentimeter: Great for Presentations, but with Limitations

Mentimeter is a Swedish tool best known for interactive presentations. Word clouds, polls, quiz elements — it's all there. The interface is polished and the design is appealing.

The issue: servers are located in Sweden, not Germany. While Sweden is part of the EU, different states and school authorities in Germany have varying interpretations of whether that's sufficient. On top of that, the free version is heavily restricted. For professional training with strict data protection requirements, a German hosted solution is the safer bet.

## Kahoot and Quizlet: Avoid for School Use

Both tools are well known and popular — but for use in German schools or with sensitive audiences, they simply aren't recommended. Data is stored on US servers, a DPA is difficult or impossible to obtain, and there's no real data protection concept for European educational institutions.

That doesn't mean you can never use them. For personal use or adult education without strict data protection obligations, it can be fine on a case by case basis. But for school settings or companies with serious compliance requirements, steer clear.
